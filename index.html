Polymers

Long-chain molecules, and the properties that follow

07 Mn · Mw · PDI

Molecular weight is a distribution

Build a polymer as a mixture of chain lengths and watch two averages pull apart: the number-average weights every chain equally, while the weight-average leans toward the long-chain tail, since longer chains carry more of the mass. Drag any chain, drop in one giant chain, and see why the polydispersity index — the ratio of the two — equals exactly 1 only when every chain is identical.

08 MW · Xc · Xlink · branch

Four levers, one exception

Toggle four structural levers — molecular weight, crystallinity, crosslinking, and branching — across preset polymer types (HDPE-like, LDPE-like, a thermoset) and watch stiffness, strength, and Tg/Tm respond. Three levers push every property the same direction; branching is the outlier, loosening chain packing to pull all three back down.

Diffusion

How atoms move through a solid — and how far they get

09 vacancy · interstitial

Two ways an atom moves

Run the two diffusion mechanisms side by side: a substitutional atom that can only jump when a vacancy sits next door, and a small interstitial that squeezes through the gaps at every attempt. Turn up the temperature to raise the vacancy supply and watch the hop counters diverge — interstitial diffusion pulls far ahead.

10 random walk · √N

Why depth grows as √(Dt)

Release a swarm of atoms taking random fixed-length jumps and watch their average net displacement track √N, not N — each atom wanders rather than marches. That gap is exactly why diffusion depth scales as √(Dt) instead of linearly with time.

11 ln D vs 1/T

Temperature is everything

Drag the temperature and watch the diffusivity swing by orders of magnitude for a modest change, because ln D vs 1/T is a straight line whose slope is −Q/R, fixed by the activation energy for diffusion. Switch the diffusing species to compare a shallow interstitial line against steep self-diffusion, and read off how far carbon travels in four hours.

12 Fick's 2nd · erf

Carburizing: the √(Dt) rule

Push carbon into γ-iron and watch the concentration profile follow Fick's second law, an error-function curve set by the surface concentration. Slide time and temperature to see the case depth grow as √(Dt) — doubling the depth costs four times the time — while temperature moves D exponentially and dominates every other lever.

Mechanical behavior & strengthening

The stress–strain curve, and the dislocations behind every number

13 elastic · yield · fracture

Every property is a point on the curve

Drag a marker along the engineering stress–strain curve and watch the atomic panel show what's happening — bond stretch, dislocation glide, chain uncoiling, or rupture — while each property landmark (modulus, yield, tensile strength, fracture) lights up as you reach it. Switch between a ductile metal, brittle ceramic, and polymer to see how bonding sets the whole shape.

14 2 roots · 6 properties

Six properties, two roots

Mechanical properties aren't six independent facts — they fall out of two structural roots. Drag bond stiffness/strength and the ease of dislocation motion and watch the stress–strain curve reshape as stiffness, yield, tensile strength, ductility, toughness, and hardness all follow; toughness is read as the real area under the curve, so the strength–ductility trade-off is genuine.

15 τR = σ·cosφ·cosλ

What actually drives slip

A tensile pull doesn't shear a slip plane head-on — only its resolved component does. Rotate the slip plane and watch the resolved shear stress τR = σ·cosφ·cosλ peak near 45° and vanish when the plane sits square to or along the load; slip begins once it reaches the critical resolved shear stress, so the best-oriented grain yields first.

16 dislocation glide

Strength is resistance to glide

A dislocation glides easily through a perfect crystal — which is why pure metals are soft. Switch on grain boundaries, solute atoms, and a dislocation forest one at a time and watch the stress needed to keep the line moving climb; that extra stress is the added strength.

Open full ↗
17 Hall–Petch · solute · cold work

Three ways to strengthen a metal

Grain refinement, solid-solution alloying, and cold work each throw a different obstacle in a dislocation's path — Hall–Petch grain boundaries, solute strain fields, and tangled forest dislocations — so each adds to the yield strength. But watch ductility: two of the three spend it, and only grain refinement raises strength without wrecking ductility.

18 recovery · recryst. · growth

Annealing: undoing cold work

Cold work leaves a metal hard, brittle, and tangled with dislocations. Raise the annealing temperature and three processes run in sequence — recovery, recrystallization, then grain growth — trading strength back for ductility as the dislocation density falls. Drag the temperature and watch the microstructure and properties reset.

Failure & service life

From a single flaw to fracture, fatigue, and creep

19 σmax = Kₜ·σ₀

Flaws concentrate stress

The average stress on a part tells you almost nothing about where it breaks — a hole, notch, or sharp corner multiplies the local stress right at the feature. Sharpen the notch radius and watch the stress-concentration factor Kₜ and the peak stress climb without bound; it's the physics behind why designers round every corner.

Open full ↗
20 K = Yσ√(πa)

Will this crack run?

A crack turns dangerous the moment the stress intensity K = Yσ√(πa) reaches the material's fracture toughness. Move your operating point — applied stress and crack size — across the chart: the curve is the fracture boundary, safe below and running above. Three levers pull you back to safety: a tougher material, lower stress, or smaller flaws.

21 σₐ · Δσ · R

Amplitude drives fatigue

Any repeating load is fixed by just two numbers — σmax and σmin — and the mean, amplitude, range, and ratio all follow. Drag them and watch the estimated fatigue life respond: it's the amplitude σₐ that drives fatigue, and for steel there's a threshold stress below which life is effectively infinite.

22 three stages · rupture

Creep: slow stretch under heat and load

At high temperature a part under constant load keeps deforming — slowly but relentlessly, through primary, steady-state, and tertiary stages, until it ruptures. Raise the temperature or the stress and the steady-state creep rate climbs; because it's diffusion-assisted, temperature dominates (Arrhenius, as in diffusion), and the time to rupture collapses.

Heat treatment & transformations

Same steel — the cooling and tempering schedule sets the part

25 TTT · isothermal

Hold at a temperature, watch what forms

Quench eutectoid steel to a hold temperature and wait: drag the temperature line and time cursor to see transformation begin where your path crosses the start curve and finish at the dashed one. Same steel every time — a high hold gives soft coarse pearlite, a lower one hard fine pearlite or bainite, and dropping below Ms shears austenite straight to martensite with no waiting.

Open full ↗
26 CCT · cooling rate

How fast you cool decides what you get

Real parts cool continuously, not in a held step. Drag the cooling curve faster or slower — or pick furnace, air, oil, or water — and read the microstructure that results. Beat the nose of the curve (cool faster than the critical rate) and you dodge pearlite entirely for fully martensite; cool slower and diffusion has time to build pearlite before you reach Ms.

Open full ↗
27 quench · temper

Build a process, get a part

Start from red-hot austenite, choose how to cool it, then how to temper it — and watch the same steel become a soft blank, a spring, or a glass-hard file. It's the payoff of quench-and-temper: tempering buys back most of the toughness lost to a hard martensitic quench while giving up only a little hardness, the strong-and-tough sweet spot.

Open full ↗
28 aging · peak · over-age

Grow precipitates to peak strength

Solution-treat and quench an aluminium alloy, then set an aging temperature and drag the time cursor: fine coherent precipitates grow and yield strength climbs to a peak, because dislocations must bow around the closely-spaced particles. Keep aging and the particles coarsen and spread (Ostwald ripening), the gaps widen, and strength falls — over-aging is too much of a good thing.

Composites & materials selection

Combining materials — and choosing the right one for the job

29 along vs across

Fibers as springs

Drag the fiber volume fraction and watch composite stiffness split by direction. Along the fibers they act like springs in parallel, so the stiff phase wins — Ec = Vf·Ef + Vm·Em, a weighted average. Across them the soft matrix sits in the load path in series and drags stiffness down (1/Ec = Vf/Ef + Vm/Em). Swap the fiber and matrix materials and watch the anisotropy grow.

Open full ↗
30 matrix · fiber · layup

Design a laminate

Choose a matrix and fiber, set the fiber fraction, and pick a layup — unidirectional, cross-ply, or quasi-isotropic — then read stiffness, strength, specific stiffness, and weight from the rule of mixtures. All-parallel fibers give the highest stiffness and strength but only along the fibers; cross-ply and quasi-isotropic trade that peak for more even, multi-directional properties, the way a real part is matched to the loads it will see.

Open full ↗
31 E–ρ · material index

Chase the material index

Every point is a material on a stiffness-versus-density chart. Pick a design goal — a light stiff tie-rod, beam, or panel — and it sets the guideline slope; drag the selection line and the materials that stay in the upper-left beat your target. The winning index changes with the job: E/ρ for a tie-rod in tension, √E/ρ for a beam, ∛E/ρ for a panel — which is why "best material" means nothing until you name the load.
